Interpretability & XAI
The field splits into two subfields. There's traditional XAI, which focuses on explaining model outputs, mostly for deployment and auditing. And then there is Mechanistic Interpretability, which tries to reverse-engineer what's actually happening inside the model. Finish section 1 and then decide which one of these you want to commit your summers to. You can do both if you really want to though.

2. Classical XAI
Christoph Molnar: Interpretable Machine Learning https://christophm.github.io/interpretable-ml-book/
Your reference text for classical XAI. Covers LIME, SHAP, PDPs, counterfactuals blah blah.
The point is, read a chapter here, then go on to read the foundational paper of that concept. 

Here’s a rough list of Foundational papers to read alongside it:
LIME: Ribeiro et al., "Why Should I Trust You?" (2016) https://arxiv.org/abs/1602.04938 -- 
SHAP: Lundberg & Lee (2017) https://arxiv.org/abs/1705.07874
Shap is a bit hard to get at once, these https://shap.readthedocs.io/ have a lot of visual explanations that might help. 
TCAV: Kim et al. (2018) https://arxiv.org/abs/1711.11279 
Grad-CAM: Selvaraju et al. (2017) https://arxiv.org/abs/1610.02391 
Lipton, "The Mythos of Model Interpretability" (2018) https://arxiv.org/abs/1606.03490 
read this after the above four
Doshi-Velez & Kim, "Towards a Rigorous Science of Interpretable Machine Learning" (2017) https://arxiv.org/abs/1702.08608 
You would anyways realize this early on that not all explanations are equal, read through the main body of this paper as well.
    
3. Mechanistic Interpretability
Neel Nanda: A Comprehensive Mechanistic Interpretability Explainer
The below given doc is gonna be the centerpiece here. Essentially a map of the field, when you encounter a new term, for instance “sparse auto-encoder’ come back to this doc and see what it is about. https://www.neelnanda.io/mechanistic-interpretability/glossary 

You can start by reading the rest of the circuit thread’s blogs. 
Foundational papers, in order:
Elhage et al. (Anthropic), "A Mathematical Framework for Transformer Circuits" (2021) https://transformer-circuits.pub/2021/framework/index.html 
Introduces the residual stream and induction heads.
Wang et al., "Interpretability in the Wild: A Circuit for IOI in GPT-2" (2022) https://arxiv.org/abs/2211.00593 example of a finished circuit-finding project. 
Elhage et al. (Anthropic), "Toy Models of Superposition" (2022) https://transformer-circuits.pub/2022/toy_model/index.html 
Superposition introduced here. 
Bricken et al. (Anthropic), "Towards Monosemanticity" (2023) https://transformer-circuits.pub/2023/monosemanticity/index.html 
Sparse autoencoders as a tool to find interpretable features inside LLMs.
